## Crowded features

These are expected table stakes and poor standalone differentiation:

- AI destination and itinerary recommendations.
- A day-by-day list of activities.
- Packing checklists and simple expense tables.
- Saved places on a map.
- Generic all-in-one dashboards.

## Defensible gap

The strongest gap is an editable execution model:

1. Places and routes are equal timeline items.
2. Routes explicitly connect endpoint places.
3. The original plan remains visible after changes.
4. A delay can move dependent later items.
5. Constraints create understandable warnings.
6. The same stable data survives across local notes, collaborative planning,
   and the paid execution/recording service.

## Implications for launch messaging

Lead with:

- "A travel plan that understands how you get there."
- "Change today's plan without erasing the plan you started with."
- "Your itinerary data stays yours."

Do not lead with:

- "AI-powered travel planner."
- "Everything you need for travel."
- "Beautiful Notion dashboard."

Those messages place the product in the most crowded part of the market.

## Useful competitive signals

- Tripsy emphasizes forwarded booking emails, documents, QR codes, expenses,
  calendar sync, and a lifetime purchase. This validates document-at-activity
  level and non-subscription pricing.
- Tripomatic markets AI planning and offline maps, but public app feedback
  complains about missing per-stop times and rearrangement. Fine-grained
  timeline control remains valuable.
- Google My Maps is excellent for searching, importing, collaborating, and
  attaching media to places, but custom routes do not reliably become
  turn-by-turn execution plans in Google Maps.
- Paid Notion travel templates commonly sell the same itinerary, budget,
  booking, checklist, and journal bundle for low one-time prices. A free
  Travelog template must differentiate structurally, not merely include more
  databases.
